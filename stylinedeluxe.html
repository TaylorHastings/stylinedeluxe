During WWII almost every conceivable item was rationed by the United States Government. Food, personal care, paper, photo film, ink and nylon to name a few items. Many items vital to the production of automobiles was either heavily rationed or completely unavailable. Some of these items include:

  • metal
  • fuel oil
  • rubber (tires)
  • gasoline
  • grease
  • kerosene
  • industrial alcohol
  • leather
  • textiles (cloth)

In January 1942, Federal Price Administrator, Leon Henderson, ordered Detroit to cease making cars and trucks for the civilian market in order to free up assembly lines for military production. The bans start date: Feb. 1, 1942. Chevrolet builds T-17 Staghound armored scout cars in Flint, Mich., during World War II. Chevy built 3,800 Staghounds, most with the 37 mm cannons shown here, between October 1942 and April 1944.

Postwar auto production had seen manufactures issue touched-up versions of their 1942 cars.Some fresh designs had started coming out. In 1949 it was Chevrolet’s turn. In 1949, Chevrolet presented its first all new models since the end of World War II. The car had a lower, sleeker profile, with the lines of the front fenders smoothly blending into the doors to be countered by the rear fenders that continued to bulge out of the sides of the car. A two-piece curved windshield improved vision and added to the overall sleek look.There were 14 new models divided into the Fleetline and Styleline series. The Styleline was represented by notchback styles of sedans, coupes, station wagons (Chevy’s last with structural-wood construction.), and a convertible. The 1949 cars came in two trim levels: including, the nicer DeLuxe. Fender skirts were part of the DeLuxe trim package.
